▢ 01 / 05

Ensemble ML for flash-flood forecasting across the Appalachians

A three-model ensemble (XGBoost, Random Forest, LSTM) trained on up to 25 years of hourly USGS streamflow and NOAA AORC precipitation across nine gauging stations in North Carolina, Tennessee, West Virginia, and Virginia. Best-performing model achieves Nash–Sutcliffe Efficiency of 0.900 ± 0.126 at 2-hour lead and 0.785 ± 0.167 at 6-hour lead — exceeding published benchmarks for the region. SHAP interpretability reveals antecedent soil moisture proxies dominate at longer lead times.

▢ 02 / 05

Himalayan rain–snow partitioning, empirically corrected

Forty years of WFDEI reanalysis data over the Langtang watershed — a glacierized basin in the Central Himalayas — analyzed with a psychrometric energy-balance model that accounts for temperature, humidity, and wind. The empirical rain–snow transition temperature is 1.47 °C, not the 0 °C threshold assumed in nearly all prior hydrological models. Rainfall fraction shows a clear upward trend of +0.17%/year (annual daytime), with changepoints between 1990–2000.

▢ 03 / 05

Flood susceptibility for 49 ungauged Appalachian watersheds

An AI framework extending the Appalachian flash-flood ensemble to ungauged basins — those without USGS streamflow stations — using transfer learning and basin-characteristic regression. Quantifies flash-flood susceptibility at R² = 0.73 across 49 watersheds in seven U.S. states, with SHAP explainability identifying physical drivers for state and federal emergency management agencies.
